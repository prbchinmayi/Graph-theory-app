A Hamiltonian graph is a graph that contains a Hamiltonian cycle, which is a closed loop that visits every vertex of the graph exactly once before returning to the starting vertex. The concept was introduced by the mathematician Sir William Rowan Hamilton in the 19th century through a puzzle known as the “Icosian Game.” In graph theory, Hamiltonian graphs are studied extensively because they help in solving problems related to traversal, optimization, and connectivity. A graph that contains a Hamiltonian path but not a cycle is called a semi-Hamiltonian graph.

Hamiltonian graphs are different from Eulerian graphs, although both involve traversal in graphs. In an Eulerian graph, every edge must be traversed exactly once, while in a Hamiltonian graph, every vertex must be visited exactly once. This distinction makes Hamiltonian problems generally more difficult to solve. For example, a graph may be Eulerian but not Hamiltonian, or Hamiltonian but not Eulerian. Therefore, separate conditions and theorems are used to study these two types of graphs in graph theory.
Determining whether a graph is Hamiltonian is a challenging problem because no simple necessary and sufficient condition exists for all graphs. However, mathematicians have developed several useful sufficient conditions. Dirac’s Theorem states that a simple graph with (n) vertices ((n \geq 3)) is Hamiltonian if every vertex has degree at least (n/2). Another important result is Ore’s Theorem, which says that if the sum of the degrees of every pair of non-adjacent vertices is at least (n), then the graph is Hamiltonian. These theorems help identify Hamiltonian graphs without explicitly searching for cycles.

Hamiltonian graphs have important applications in many real-world fields such as computer science, transportation, logistics, and communication networks. One of the most famous applications is the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP), where a salesman must visit multiple cities exactly once and return to the original city while minimizing travel cost. Hamiltonian cycles are also useful in designing delivery routes, robotic movement planning, and integrated circuit layouts. Their ability to represent efficient traversal makes them highly valuable in optimization problems.
In computer science, Hamiltonian graph problems are closely related to algorithm design and computational complexity. Finding a Hamiltonian cycle in a graph is considered an NP-complete problem, which means that no known algorithm can solve all cases efficiently in polynomial time. As the number of vertices increases, the complexity of checking every possible cycle grows rapidly. Because of this, researchers often use heuristics, approximation algorithms, and special graph properties to solve practical Hamiltonian problems within a reasonable time.
