An Eulerian circuit is a closed trail in a graph that visits every edge exactly once, returning to the starting vertex. The concept originates from Leonhard Euler's 1736 solution to the famous Königsberg Bridge Problem, where he proved it impossible to walk across all seven bridges of Königsberg exactly once and return to the start. This landmark result is widely regarded as the birth of graph theory itself. Euler's insight was to abstract the physical map into a mathematical structure of vertices and edges, allowing rigorous analysis through combinatorial reasoning rather than exhaustive trial.

The fundamental theorem governing Eulerian circuits states that a connected graph contains an Eulerian circuit if and only if every vertex in the graph has even degree. Degree here refers to the number of edges incident to a vertex. The intuition is elegant: every time a traversal enters a vertex, it must also leave, consuming two edges per visit. If any vertex has odd degree, a traversal will eventually get "trapped" there with no unused edge to exit — making a complete circuit impossible. This necessary and sufficient condition gives a clean, efficient criterion for existence. For directed graphs, the condition adapts naturally. A connected directed graph admits an Eulerian circuit if and only if every vertex has equal in-degree and out-degree. This ensures that for every edge used to arrive at a vertex, a corresponding outgoing edge exists to continue the traversal, maintaining the closed-loop property throughout the entire graph.

Constructive algorithms exist to actually find Eulerian circuits when they are known to exist. Hierholzer's algorithm is the most efficient, operating in O(E) time. It works by repeatedly extending partial circuits: start a cycle from any vertex, then splice in new cycles at vertices that still have unused edges, until all edges are exhausted. Fleury's algorithm offers an alternative, though it is slower due to its bridge-avoidance checks at each step.Eulerian circuits have rich practical applications. They appear in route optimization problems, DNA fragment assembly in bioinformatics, VLSI circuit design, and the Chinese Postman Problem, which seeks the shortest closed walk covering every edge in a weighted graph. Understanding Eulerian structure also underpins the study of Hamiltonian cycles, de Bruijn sequences, and network flow theory, making it one of the most foundational ideas in combinatorics and discrete mathematics.
