The Havel–Hakimi algorithm is a fundamental procedure in graph theory used to determine whether a given sequence of non-negative integers can be realized as the degree sequence of a simple undirected graph. A sequence is called graphical if there exists at least one graph in which each vertex has the degree specified by the sequence. Instead of attempting to construct all possible graphs, the algorithm provides a systematic way to verify the feasibility of such a sequence through iterative reduction.

The algorithm begins by arranging the degree sequence in non-increasing order. This ensures that the largest degree is always considered first, which is crucial because the vertex with the highest degree must be connected to the maximum number of other vertices. By always working with a sorted sequence, the algorithm maintains consistency and avoids ambiguity in choosing which degrees to reduce during each iteration.
In each step, the algorithm removes the largest degree (d) from the sequence and then reduces the next (d) largest degrees by 1. This operation represents connecting the corresponding vertex to (d) other vertices in the graph. After performing this reduction, the sequence is again sorted in non-increasing order before proceeding to the next iteration. This repetitive process mimics the gradual construction of a graph while maintaining the degree requirements.

During execution, two important failure conditions are checked. First, if the removed degree (d) is greater than the number of remaining elements in the sequence, it is impossible to connect the vertex to enough distinct vertices, and the sequence is declared non-graphical. Second, if any degree becomes negative after subtraction, it indicates that more connections are being forced than allowed, which also makes the sequence invalid. These checks ensure that the algorithm terminates early in infeasible cases.

If the algorithm continues successfully until all elements of the sequence are reduced to zero, then the sequence is confirmed to be graphical. This means that a simple graph exists that satisfies the given degree constraints. In fact, the steps of the algorithm can also be used to explicitly construct such a graph by keeping track of which vertices are connected at each stage.
The Havel–Hakimi algorithm is important because it provides an efficient and intuitive alternative to brute-force methods of graph construction. It is widely used in areas such as network analysis, combinatorics, and computer science, especially in problems involving degree sequences and graph realization. Moreover, it helps build a deeper understanding of how local constraints on vertex degrees influence the overall structure of a graph.
