A graph in computer science is a collection of vertices (nodes) connected by edges, where each edge carries a numerical value called a weight. In many real-world problems such as designing road networks, laying electrical cables, or building communication systems, the goal is to connect all points together at the lowest possible total cost. This is exactly the problem that the Minimum Spanning Tree solves.

A Minimum Spanning Tree, commonly abbreviated as MST, is a subset of edges selected from a connected, undirected, weighted graph such that all vertices remain connected, no cycles are formed, and the sum of the selected edge weights is as small as possible. Since the selected edges form a tree, a graph with V vertices will always have exactly V minus 1 edges in its MST. If a graph has multiple edges with equal weights, there may be more than one valid MST, but all of them will share the same minimum total weight.

There are two classical algorithms for finding the MST, namely Prim's algorithm and Kruskal's algorithm. Both are greedy algorithms, meaning they make the locally best choice at each step with the expectation that this leads to the globally best solution. Kruskal's algorithm, proposed by Joseph Kruskal in 1956, takes an edge-centric approach and is particularly elegant in its simplicity.
The fundamental intuition behind Kruskal's algorithm is straightforward. If you sort all edges from cheapest to most expensive and keep adding edges one by one, always skipping any edge that would create a cycle, you will eventually end up with the minimum spanning tree. The greedy choice here is always picking the smallest available edge, and the constraint is that it must not connect two vertices that are already in the same connected component, because doing so would form a cycle.

The algorithm proceeds in three broad phases. In the first phase, all edges in the graph are sorted in non-decreasing order of their weights. This sorted list becomes the order in which edges are considered. In the second phase, a data structure called Union-Find, also known as Disjoint Set Union or DSU, is initialized. Each vertex is placed into its own separate set, meaning initially every vertex is its own isolated component with no connections. In the third phase, the algorithm iterates through the sorted edge list. For each edge connecting vertices U and V, it checks whether U and V belong to different components. If they do, the edge is safe to add because it connects two previously disconnected parts of the graph without creating a cycle. The two components are then merged into one. If U and V already belong to the same component, adding the edge would create a cycle, so it is discarded. This continues until exactly V minus 1 edges have been added, at which point all vertices are connected and the MST is complete.
