In graph theory, a walk is the most general form of traversal. A walk in a graph is a sequence of vertices and edges where each consecutive pair of vertices is connected by an edge, with no restrictions whatsoever on repetition. Both vertices and edges may be revisited any number of times during a walk. A trail is a walk with the additional constraint that no edge may be repeated, though vertices may still be revisited freely. A path imposes the strictest conditions, requiring that neither edges nor vertices be repeated. A path is thus a trail where all intermediate vertices are also distinct. 

A closed walk is simply a walk that begins and ends at the same vertex, with no further restrictions on edge or vertex repetition. Closed walks are significant in spectral graph theory, where the trace of matrix powers counts them systematically. Specifically, the trace of A^k gives the total number of closed walks of length k in the graph, summed over all starting vertices. This connection between closed walks and eigenvalues of the adjacency matrix is foundational to spectral methods, linking combinatorial structure to linear algebraic properties in a deep and computationally useful way.

A closed trail, also called a circuit, is a closed walk in which no edge is repeated, though vertices other than the start and end may recur. Circuits are central objects in graph theory and appear in the cycle space of a graph, which is a vector space over the field with two elements capturing all possible edge-disjoint combinations of circuits. Every graph's cycle space has a well-defined dimension equal to the circuit rank, given by the formula E − V + C, where E is the number of edges, V the number of vertices, and C the number of connected components. Eulerian circuits are the special circuits that exhaust every edge of the graph exactly once, sitting at the intersection of circuit theory and covering problems.

A closed path, more commonly called a cycle, is a closed walk where no vertex repeats except for the mandatory repetition of the start and end vertex. Cycles are among the most studied structures in all of graph theory. A graph containing no cycles is called a tree if connected, or a forest if possibly disconnected, and these acyclic structures have special properties including exactly V−1 edges for a tree on V vertices. The girth of a graph is the length of its shortest cycle, and the circumference is the length of its longest. Cycle structure governs planarity through Kuratowski's theorem, graph coloring through odd-cycle conditions, and network reliability through independent cycle analysis, making cycles indispensable across virtually every branch of graph theory.
