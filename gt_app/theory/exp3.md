A subgraph is a fundamental concept in graph theory, referring to a graph formed from a subset of the vertices and edges of a larger graph. Given a graph (G = (V, E)), a subgraph (H = (V', E')) is defined such that (V' \subseteq V) and (E' \subseteq E), with every edge in (E') connecting vertices that are present in (V'). Subgraphs help in analyzing smaller portions of a graph without losing the structural relationships between selected vertices and edges.
A special type of subgraph is the spanning subgraph, which includes all the vertices of the original graph but only a subset of its edges. In other words, for a spanning subgraph (H), we have (V' = V) but (E' \subseteq E). These subgraphs are important because they preserve the entire set of vertices while simplifying connectivity. A common example is a spanning tree, which connects all vertices with the minimum number of edges and contains no cycles.
Another important category is the node-induced subgraph (also called a vertex-induced subgraph). Given a subset of vertices (V' \subseteq V), the node-induced subgraph includes all edges from the original graph that connect pairs of vertices in (V'). This means the edge set is automatically determined by the chosen vertices. Node-induced subgraphs are useful when the focus is on a specific group of vertices and their inherent connections within the larger graph.
In contrast, an edge-induced subgraph is formed by selecting a subset of edges (E' \subseteq E), and then including only those vertices that are endpoints of the selected edges. Unlike node-induced subgraphs, here the vertex set depends on the chosen edges. This type of subgraph is useful when analyzing specific relationships or interactions represented by particular edges, regardless of the full set of vertices in the original graph.
These different types of subgraphs provide flexibility in studying graphs from multiple perspectives. Spanning subgraphs emphasize connectivity across all vertices, node-induced subgraphs focus on relationships within a chosen group of vertices, and edge-induced subgraphs highlight specific connections or interactions. Each type serves a different purpose depending on the problem being analyzed.
