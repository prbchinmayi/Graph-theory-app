The study of graph isomorphism is founded on the principle of structural identity, wherein two graphs are regarded as equivalent if they exhibit identical connectivity, irrespective of differences in visual representation or vertex labeling. Formally, an isomorphism between two graphs (G_1) and (G_2) is defined as a bijective function that maps the vertices of (G_1) to those of (G_2) while strictly preserving adjacency relationships. This condition guarantees that an edge exists between any two vertices in the first graph if and only if a corresponding edge exists between their mapped counterparts in the second graph. This concept serves as a fundamental element of algebraic graph theory, as it enables the classification of graphs into equivalence classes whose members share identical structural characteristics such as connectivity, diameter, and chromatic number. From a mathematical standpoint, graph isomorphism is categorized as an equivalence relation governed by the essential properties of reflexivity, symmetry, and transitivity.

Reflexivity asserts that every graph is inherently isomorphic to itself through an identity mapping, commonly termed a trivial automorphism. Symmetry ensures that if an isomorphic mapping exists from (G_1) to (G_2), then a corresponding inverse mapping must exist from (G_2) to (G_1), thereby establishing mutual equivalence. Transitivity extends this principle by stating that if (G_1) is isomorphic to (G_2), and (G_2) is isomorphic to (G_3), then (G_1) must necessarily be isomorphic to (G_3). Collectively, these properties provide a robust logical framework that supports the systematic development of algorithms for verifying the equivalence of complex graph structures. Such a framework is indispensable for theoretical investigations and computational procedures aimed at analyzing structural similarities across diverse networked systems.

To computationally determine whether two graphs are isomorphic, the analysis typically involves the comparison of graph invariants—properties that remain unchanged under any valid isomorphic mapping. The most fundamental invariants include the order of a graph, defined as the number of vertices, and its size, represented by the number of edges. Any discrepancy in these parameters immediately eliminates the possibility of isomorphism. A more refined invariant is the degree sequence, which is the sorted list of vertex degrees and must be identical for both graphs under consideration. Nevertheless, identical degree sequences alone do not guarantee isomorphism, as the overall topology of a graph depends on the precise manner in which vertices of varying degrees are interconnected. Consequently, deeper structural analysis is required to confirm equivalence beyond these preliminary checks.
